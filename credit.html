What builds your score — and where you stand
The most important factor: Payment history (35% of your score). One missed payment can drop your score by 60–100 points. Always pay at least the minimum — ideally the full balance.
Your month-by-month credit journey
The 3 rules that matter most
30%
Utilisation limit
Never use more than 30% of your credit limit. If your limit is $500, keep balance under $150. This single rule has the biggest impact after payment history.
100%
Pay in full
Pay your full balance every month — not just the minimum. Minimum payments keep you in debt and cost you interest. Full payment = zero interest + score improvement.
6mo
Before applying again
Every credit application creates a "hard inquiry" that drops your score slightly. Wait 6 months between applications. Don't apply for multiple cards at once.
Which cards to get — in order
Newcomer tip: All major Canadian banks (RBC, TD, Scotiabank, BMO, CIBC) have newcomer banking packages that include a secured credit card with no credit history required. Go to a branch — don't apply online for your first card. Bring your passport, SIN, and work permit or PR card.
Mistakes that set you back months
✗
Applying for too many cards at once. Each application is a hard inquiry on your credit report. Three applications in one month signals desperation to lenders and drops your score. One card at a time, 6 months apart.
✗
Only paying the minimum balance. The credit card company loves this — you pay interest on the rest. Your score improves faster if you pay in full. Minimum payments also keep your utilisation high.
✗
Closing your first credit card. Your oldest card is your longest credit history — it matters. Even after you get better cards, keep your first secured card open with occasional small purchases.
✗
Thinking rent payments build credit automatically. In Canada, rent does NOT appear on your credit report by default. Ask your landlord to use a rent reporting service (Landlord Credit Bureau) or use a credit-building app like Borrowell Rent Advantage.
✗
Not checking your credit report. Errors on credit reports are more common than you think. Check your free report at Equifax and TransUnion once a year. Dispute any errors immediately — even small ones affect your score.
