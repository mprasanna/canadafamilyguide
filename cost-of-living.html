The Cornwall surprise
Cornwall rent for families is closer to Ottawa than you'd expect
Headlines say Cornwall is one of Ontario's cheapest cities to rent. That's true for apartments — the average 1–2 bedroom here is around $1,500/month. But if you need a family-sized home, the math changes fast. A 4-bedroom semi-detached in Cornwall rents for $2,500–2,800. An equivalent home in Ottawa is $2,600–2,900. The difference for families isn't the 50% savings most newcomers expect — it's maybe $100–300 a month. Cornwall still wins on total cost of living (property taxes, food, activities all trend lower), but on rent alone, the gap for family homes is small.

City by city

What the trade-offs actually look like

Each city buys you something different. Here's the honest read — not the tourism board version.

Cornwall
Eastern Ontario · small city · slower pace
~$6,100/mo
Midpoint, family of four
What you get: a bigger house for less, quieter neighbourhoods, school boards with small class sizes, and a 45-minute drive to Ottawa or Montreal when you need a bigger city. What you trade: limited restaurants, no Costco in town (so you drive to Ottawa or Vaudreuil), smaller job market, fewer cultural activities for kids.
Best fit: families who value space over proximity — especially if you work remotely or commute to Ottawa.
Ottawa
National capital · mid-size · balanced
~$6,250/mo
Midpoint, family of four
What you get: federal jobs, bilingual schools, strong tech sector, real winters but real infrastructure, and surprisingly walkable neighbourhoods. What you trade: higher car insurance and groceries than Cornwall, family homes rent similarly to Cornwall but condos/apartments cost more.
Best fit: newcomer families who want a full-city experience without Toronto's costs — strong balance point.
Toronto
Canada's biggest city · everything is here
~$7,600/mo
Midpoint, family of four
What you get: the largest job market in Canada, every ethnic grocery and cultural community you could want, top-tier schools, and transit that actually works. What you trade: significantly higher rent, the highest car insurance in Canada, and a commute culture that eats your evenings.
Best fit: families with both parents in specialised careers, who value access over affordability.

What newcomers miss
Most listings show rent "plus utilities". That means you add hydro, gas, and water on top — typically another $250–400/month depending on season. Always ask what's included before signing. A "$2,500 rent" house often costs $2,800+ once you count the bills.

The hot water tank rental trap
Most Ontario rental homes come with a rented hot water tank — $30–45/month, billed on your Enbridge gas bill, forever. It's legal and common, but many newcomers don't realise it's there. You can buy out the contract and purchase your own tank (~$1,500 upfront), which pays back in 3–4 years. Worth checking during your first month. Read your gas bill — the "Water Heater Rental" line is where it hides.

The Cornwall Costco problem
Cornwall doesn't have a Costco. The nearest ones are Ottawa (~1 hr drive), Vaudreuil, QC (~50 min), or Montreal (~1 hr 20 min). Many Cornwall families do a monthly "Costco run" and stock up — not an inconvenience once you plan it, but something to factor into your fuel and time budget. A round trip is ~$30–40 in gas.

A car is not optional in Cornwall
Cornwall has limited transit — a car is essentially mandatory for most working adults. Ottawa has decent transit (OC Transpo + O-Train) and many families manage with one car. Toronto is the only city where two parents can both work without owning a car, thanks to the TTC subway. Factor this into the total picture — a second car in Ontario adds $400–700/month all-in (insurance + fuel + maintenance + plates).

The honest math for two kids
If each kid does 3 activities at middle-of-the-road pricing (say $130 avg), that's $130 × 3 × 2 kids = $780/month. Four activities each pushes it past $1,000/month. This is before equipment, costumes, registration fees, recital tickets, and summer camps (which can run $200–400/week in July/August). Kids' activities are easily the 3rd-largest line item after rent and groceries — plan for it from day one.
💼 What this means for your salary

Translating total monthly costs into household income. Assumes combined gross household salary (both partners), Ontario tax rates, and CPP/EI deductions. Numbers are before retirement savings, RESP contributions, or emergency fund — so "comfortable" really means comfortable.

Tight
$75–95K
You'll make it work, but it's tight. Cornwall is doable at this level; Ottawa manageable with compromises; Toronto requires a smaller home or apartment. No big savings, limited kids' activities, careful grocery shopping.
Manageable
$100–140K
You can breathe. All three cities work. Kids can do 2–3 activities each. You can contribute to TFSA and save for a house. Vacations possible but not lavish. This is where most dual-income immigrant professionals land.
Comfortable
$160K+
Full quality of life. Toronto becomes easy; Cornwall/Ottawa feel abundant. Max out TFSAs and RRSPs, full RESPs for kids, private school possible, international travel, downpayment for a home in 2–3 years.
A reality check for newcomers
These salary bands are combined household income, not single income. A single-income family needs 20–30% more to hit the same comfort level because of how Ontario's tax brackets work. This is why so many newcomer families target two working adults within the first year — not because they love working, but because the math basically demands it.
